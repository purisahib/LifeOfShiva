# ARTICLE 1: SAMUDRA MANTHANA (Churning of the Cosmic Ocean)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samudra_manthan

## Nomenclature

The churning of the ocean episode has three primary Sanskrit designations. "Sāgara manthana" uses the term sāgara, which is synonymous with samudra, both denoting a sea or expansive water body. "Kshirasāgara manthana" specifically references the ocean of milk, combining kshira (milk) with sāgara (ocean). "Amrita Manthana" emphasizes the elixir component, translating as "Churning for the Elixir."

## Legend

The narrative begins with Indra, King of Svarga, encountering the sage Durvasa, who offered him a special garland from an apsara. Indra placed the garland on his elephant Airavata's trunk as a sign of humility. However, bees attracted by the flowers annoyed the elephant, which threw the garland to the ground. Durvasa became enraged because the garland housed Sri (Lakshmi), goddess of fortune, and should have been treated as a sacred offering. The goddess subsequently vanished into the oceans, and Durvasa cursed Indra and all devas to lose their strength, energy, and fortune.

Following this curse, the devas faced defeat in battle. The asuras, led by Bali, gained dominion over the three worlds. The desperate devas sought Vishnu's counsel. Vishnu advised diplomatic engagement and proposed a joint venture with the asuras to churn the ocean for the nectar of immortality, promising to ensure the devas would ultimately obtain it exclusively.

### The Churning Process

Mount Mandara was uprooted to serve as the churning rod. Vasuki, a naga residing on Shiva's neck, became the churning rope after receiving assurance of his share. During this massive undertaking, many devas and asuras perished from exhaustion. Vishnu, riding Garuda, revived the fallen beings and transported Mandara to the ocean's center.

Once positioned, Vasuki coiled around the mountain. Vishnu instructed the devas to hold the serpent's head while the asuras held the tail. The asuras, deeming this inauspicious, refused and reversed positions. The churning began, but Mandara proved too heavy and sank. Vishnu assumed his Kurma (turtle) form to support the mountain on his shell.

During the churning, the lethal poison halahala emerged from Vasuki's mouth. This terrifyingly potent toxin threatened all creation. The asuras suffered from toxic fumes. Despite this danger, the devas and asuras continued alternating pulls on the serpent, causing the mountain to rotate and churn the ocean. Shiva consumed the poison to protect the three worlds, which gave his throat a characteristic blue hue, earning him the epithet "Neelakantha" (the blue-throated one).

## Ratnas (Treasures)

The churning produced fourteen ratnas (gems), though scriptural lists vary between nine and fourteen items. These treasures were distributed among Shiva, Vishnu, the Maharishis, the devas, and the asuras according to their quality.

### Goddesses Emerged

Three categories of goddesses appeared from the ocean:

**Lakshmi** emerged as the goddess of prosperity and wealth, choosing Vishnu as her eternal consort.

**Apsaras** included divine damsels such as Rambha, Menaka, and Punjisthala, who selected the Gandharvas as their companions.

**Varuni** appeared as the goddess of wine (sura) and the virgin daughter of Varuna, accepted by the devas. Some scholars attribute the terminology distinguishing devas as "suras" and asuras as "those without sura" to her acceptance.

### Supernatural Animals

Three supernatural animals emerged:

**Kamadhenu** (also called Surabhi), the wish-granting cow, went to Brahma, who distributed her to sages so her ghee could be used in yajnas and rituals.

**Airavata** and other elephants were claimed by Indra.

**Uchhaishravas**, the divine seven-headed horse, was given to Bali.

### Valuables

**Kaustubha**, described as the most valuable divine jewel in the universe, was claimed by Vishnu.

**Kalpavriksha**, a divine wish-fulfilling tree with eternally blooming flowers that never fade, was taken to Indraloka by the devas.

### Additional Treasures

**Chandra** (the crescent moon) was claimed by Shiva.

**Dhanvantari** emerged as the "vaidya of the devas" carrying amrita, the nectar of immortality. (Sometimes considered as two separate ratnas.)

**Halahala**, the poison, was consumed by Shiva.

Supplementary items completing various lists include:

- **Panchajanya**: Vishnu's conch
- **Jyestha** (Alakshmi): the goddess of misfortune
- The umbrella claimed by Varuna
- Earrings given to Aditi by her son Indra
- Nidradevi, goddess of sleep

Different Puranas, the Ramayana, and the Mahabharata present variations of this list.

## Amrita (Nectar of Immortality)

Finally, Dhanvantari emerged bearing a pot containing amṛta, the heavenly nectar of immortality. The asuras and devas fought for possession. The asuras seized the nectar and fled.

The devas appealed to Vishnu, who assumed the form of Mohini, an enchantingly beautiful damsel. She captivated the asuras and arranged them in separate rows from the devas, distributing the nectar exclusively to the devas, who consumed it. An asura named Svarbhanu disguised himself as a deva and drank some nectar. The luminous deities Surya (sun) and Chandra (moon) detected this deception and informed Mohini. She severed his head with her Sudarshana Chakra. Thereafter, his head became Rahu and his body Ketu, explaining the Hindu astronomical mythology of eclipses. Once all devas received their portions, Vishnu resumed his true form and departed on Garuda to his realm.

Realizing their deception, the asuras engaged the strengthened devas in combat. The following battles ensued: Indra fought Bali, Kartikeya fought Taraka, Varuna fought Heti, Yama fought Kalanabha, Brihaspati fought Sukra, Mitra fought Praheti, Vishvakarma fought Maya, Vrishapati fought Jambha, Shani fought Naraka, Savitri fought Vilochana, Chandra fought Rahu, Vayu fought Puloman, and Aparajita fought Namuchi.

Group conflicts also occurred: the Ashvini twins versus Vrishaparva, Surya versus Bali's hundred sons, Brahma's sons versus Ilvala and Vatapi, the Maruts versus the Nivatakavacha, Kali versus Shumba and Nishumba, the Rudras versus the Krodavasas, and the Vasus versus the Kaleyas.

Rejuvenated by amrita, the devas achieved victory and banished the asuras to Patalaloka, reclaiming Svarga.

### Origin of the Kumbha Mela

Medieval Hindu theology extends this legend by asserting that while asuras transported the amṛta away, nectar drops fell at four distinct terrestrial locations: Haridwar, Prayaga (Prayagraj), Trimbak (Nashik district), and Ujjain. According to the tradition, these sites acquired mystical and spiritual significance. The Kumbha Mela, celebrated every twelve years at these locations, commemorates this event. Devotees believe bathing during the Kumbha Mela enables one to attain moksha.

However, scholars including R. B. Bhattacharya, D. P. Dubey, and Kama Maclean note that ancient texts, including various Puranas, make no mention of amṛta spilling at four locations or of the Kumbha Mela itself. These scholars contend that the Samudra Manthana legend was applied to the Kumbha Mela relatively recently to provide scriptural authority.

### Association with Mandar Parvat

Mandar Parvat, located in Banka district of Bihar, is believed by local populations to be the actual Samudra Manthana site. Regional legends identify this hill as Mount Mandara, the mountain used in the ocean churning. A nearby pond called Paapharni is associated with the nectar that emerged. Devotees believe all fourteen divine treasures appeared at this location.

## Comparative Study

Georges Dumézil conducted a comparative analysis of this episode, dubiously connecting it to various historical "Indo-European" facts and the European medieval Holy Grail legend. He reconstructed a proto-story called the "ambrosia cycle" or "cycle of the mead," featuring a theoretical trickster deity stealing immortality drink for humanity but failing to free humans from death. Dumézil subsequently abandoned this theory.

Jarich Oosten later adopted core elements, proposing "similarities" with the Old Norse poem Hymiskviða. In this narrative, beer is prepared for gods by the Jotunn sea god Ægir after Thor and Tyr recover a giant kettle. Oosten suggests the serpent Jörmungandr parallels Vasuki, though their narrative roles are substantially dissimilar.

# ARTICLE 2: HALAHALA — THE COSMIC POISON (Nilakantha / Neelakantha)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halahala
Note: The dedicated Nilakantha Wikipedia article redirects to the main Shiva article. This Halahala article covers the same story in detail.

**Halāhala** (Sanskrit हलाहल) or **Kālakūṭa** (Sanskrit कालकूट, meaning "poison of death") represents a significant poison in Hindu mythology, emerging during the churning of the Ocean of Milk when devas and asuras sought amrita, the nectar of immortality.

## Origin and Creation

During Samudra Manthana, fourteen treasures were recovered and distributed between the two clans. However, before amrita could form, the lethal poison Halāhala emerged, producing toxic fumes that caused both divine and demonic forces to collapse from asphyxiation. Desperate for assistance, both groups appealed to Brahma, who directed them to seek Shiva's help at Mount Kailash.

## Shiva's Consumption

Shiva chose to consume the entire poison himself. The goddess Sati, alarmed by this action, "gripped her husband's neck with both hands in order to stop the poison from reaching his stomach," earning Shiva the epithet Viṣakaṇṭha (poison-holder in neck). The poison permanently turned his throat blue, establishing his alternate name Nīlakaṇṭha (blue-throated one).

## Alternative Versions

Different mythological accounts present varying narratives. According to the Rigveda and certain Mahabharata editions, Vayu (wind god) reduced the poison's potency by rubbing it, then consumed most of it while Shiva drank a portion. Another variant describes Shiva consuming the poison of Vasuki, the serpent king and Shiva's familiar, who served as the churning instrument during the ocean's churning episode.

## Key Names and Epithets Arising from This Event

- **Nīlakaṇṭha** — "blue-throated one" (नीलकण्ठ) — the most famous epithet from this episode
- **Viṣakaṇṭha** — "poison-holder in neck"
- The event establishes Shiva as the supreme protector who sacrifices his own comfort for the welfare of all creation (lokasangraha)

## Supplementary Detail (from Samudra Manthana article)

The poison halahala emerged from Vasuki's mouth during the churning. This terrifyingly potent toxin threatened all creation. The asuras suffered from toxic fumes. Despite this danger, the devas and asuras continued alternating pulls on the serpent, causing the mountain to rotate and churn the ocean. Shiva consumed the poison to protect the three worlds, which gave his throat a characteristic blue hue.

In the Shiva Purana account, when the poison appeared, all the devas and asuras fled in terror. They went to Brahma, who directed them to Shiva. Shiva, moved by compassion for all living beings, agreed to drink the deadly Halahala. Parvati (in some versions: Sati), seeing her husband about to consume the poison, clasped his throat with her hands to prevent it from descending into his stomach. The poison lodged in his throat, turning it permanently blue. This act of selfless courage made Shiva revered across all three worlds as Maheshwara — the supreme lord who gives even his own body for others' protection.

# ARTICLE 3: JALANDHARA

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jalandhara

**Jalandhara** is an asura (demon) in Hindu mythology. The Sanskrit name जलन्धर (Jalandhara) means "he who holds water," and he is also known as Chalantarana, meaning "he who walks and swims."

## Birth

According to the Shiva Purana, Jalandhara was created when the god Shiva opened his third eye in fury. The incident occurred when Indra and Brihaspati traveled to Mount Kailasha to meet Shiva. A naked yogi with matted hair blocked their path — this was Shiva in disguise, testing their knowledge. When Indra threatened the yogi with his thunderbolt without recognizing him, Shiva became enraged. His third eye opened, nearly killing Indra. To prevent Indra's death, Shiva redirected the fire from his eye toward the ocean, where it transformed into a boy.

Brahma descended and predicted that the boy would become emperor of the asuras and could only be killed by Shiva himself. He would eventually return to Shiva's third eye after death.

## Ascent to Power

Jalandhara's childhood was marked by supernatural abilities. "Borne up by the wind, he flew over the ocean; his pets were lions that he had caught; and the largest birds and fishes were subject to him."

## Training and Early Life

Shukra, the guru of asuras, made Jalandhara emperor. He married Vrinda, daughter of the asura Kalanemi, and ruled with justice and nobility.

A conflict arose when Jalandhara believed the devas had treacherously taken his father Varuna's treasures. He demanded their return through a messenger named Ghasmara, but Indra refused. A fierce battle ensued between devas and asuras, with Shukra reviving dead asuras and Brihaspati reviving dead devas using medicinal herbs from Drona mountain.

Following Shukra's counsel, Jalandhara hurled the Drona mountain into the ocean to prevent the devas' revival. Desperate devas sought Vishnu's aid. During combat between Jalandhara and Vishnu, Vishnu granted the asura a boon of his choice. Jalandhara requested that Vishnu remain in his city with his followers and wife Lakshmi. Without divine support, the devas were defeated, and Jalandhara became lord of the three worlds — Svarga (heaven), Bhuloka (earth), and Patala (underworld). He confiscated all jewels hoarded by devas and gandharvas, ruling virtuously with no sickness or poverty in his realm.

## War with Shiva

The defeated devas remained unhappy and dejected. The divine sage Narada consulted them and visited Jalandhara, describing the beauty of Kailasha, Shiva's residence. Jalandhara displayed his riches, but Narada criticized him for not having the most beautiful woman as his wife, then praised Parvati's beauty.

Jalandhara sent messenger Rahu to Shiva with an insulting demand: "How can you live on alms and yet keep the beautiful Parvati? Give her to me, and wander from house to house with your alms bowl." Shiva's anger manifested as a fearsome creature called Kīrttimukha, which nearly killed Rahu.

War commenced when Jalandhara marched to Kailasha, finding Shiva positioned near Lake Manasa. Shiva's bull Nandi caused significant destruction among Jalandhara's forces. Parvati urged Shiva to enter battle. Before departing, Shiva warned Parvati to be cautious, as asuras might visit in disguise. Accompanied by Virabhadra and Manibhadra (manifestations of his anger), Shiva engaged Jalandhara's forces.

Kartikeya fought Jalandhara and was defeated. Ganesha attempted to attack but was badly defeated and left unconscious. Jalandhara created an illusion that distracted his army but not himself. He then disguised himself as Shiva and approached Parvati to deceive her.

Parvati recognized the deception and fled, enraged. She meditated on Vishnu and demanded he deceive Vrinda, Jalandhara's wife, as the asura had attempted to trick her: "He himself has shown the path. Know that to be the way in the same manner. At my bidding, make the chastity of his wife violated. O Viṣṇu, that great Daitya cannot be killed otherwise."

Vishnu caused Vrinda to dream that Shiva had killed Jalandhara. Posing as an ascetic, he created the illusion of restoring Jalandhara to life. Delighted, Vrinda spent days with him in the forest. She eventually recognized Vishnu's deception and cursed him that someone would trick his wife, that he would wander in distress with Shesha (Lakshmana), and that he would seek help from monkeys (vanaras) — prophecies fulfilled when Ravana kidnapped Sita. Vrinda then immolated herself, her soul merging with Parvati.

## Death

Learning of Vrinda's death, Jalandhara returned to Mount Kailasha, enraged, to continue the battle. As the illusion ended, Shiva's forces perceived the truth. Shiva defeated Shumbha and Nishumbha, who subsequently fled. Parvati later killed them.

Jalandhara engaged Shiva directly in final combat. When most of his army was slaughtered, Shiva beheaded him with a chakra (discus) created from his toe. Upon death, Jalandhara's soul merged with Shiva, just as Vrinda's had merged with Parvati.

# ARTICLE 4: ANDHAKA

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andhaka

## Overview

Andhaka represents a malevolent asura in Hindu mythology whose "pride is vanquished by Shiva and Parvati." The Sanskrit name literally means "He who darkens." His narrative appears across multiple sacred texts including the Matsya Purāṇa, Kūrma Purāṇa, Liṅga Purāṇa, Padma Purana, and Shiva Purana.

Physical descriptions vary across sources. One account describes him possessing "one thousand heads, and one thousand arms, and also having two thousand eyes," while alternative versions cite "two thousand arms, and two thousand legs."

## Birth and Origins

### Shiva Purana Account

The most prominent narrative describes Andhaka's creation during Shiva's meditation on Mount Mandara. When Parvati playfully covered Shiva's eyes, the universe fell into darkness. Perspiration from her hands — caused by contact with his powerful third eye — fell to earth and generated a blind boy with a disturbing appearance.

Though Shiva claimed paternity, the demon king Hiraṇyākṣa requested and received the child as his own son, naming him Andhaka due to his blindness.

### Alternative Origins

The Harivaṃśa Purāṇa presents a different genealogy, describing Andhaka as a Daitya fathered by the sage Kaśyapa and Diti. Born with "a thousand eyes and limbs," he paradoxically walked like a blind person, thereby acquiring his name.

## Power and Boons

Following Hiraṇyākṣa's death at Varaha's hands, Andhaka ascended as asura king. He subsequently performed severe penance to please Brahmā, who granted him divine vision and immortality, with the caveat that only Shiva possessed the power to kill him.

## Conflict with Shiva

### The Abduction Attempt

Andhaka eventually learned of Parvati's extraordinary beauty and, unaware of her true identity as his actual mother, determined to abduct her. This triggered an extended conflict with Shiva spanning centuries in certain accounts.

### The Blood Multiplication Problem

A critical recurring element involves Andhaka's blood. Whenever Shiva wounded him, drops falling to earth spawned duplicate demons. To counter this, "Vishnu created several Mātṛkās to drink the demon's blood every time he was hurt, preventing the spread of new demons."

### Resolution

Across most narratives, Shiva impaled Andhaka upon his trident. Following this devastating defeat and recognizing Parvati as his actual mother, Andhaka experienced profound remorse. His sins were burned away through Shiva's divine presence, prompting sincere repentance.

## Redemption and Status

Rather than destruction, Andhaka received forgiveness. Shiva "made him a Gaṇa chief" and occasionally elevated him to chief of all Gaṇas. In the Vāmana Purāṇa variant, he eventually became known as Bhṛṅgi following his rehabilitation.

## References in Other Texts

The Rāmāyaṇa briefly mentions Andhaka's death by Shiva's third eye in Chapter 30 of the Araṇya Kāṇḍa, while the Mahābhārata describes Kālī as his destroyer, though through different means than the Rāmāyaṇa account.

# ARTICLE 5: KAMADEVA

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamadeva

## Overview

Kamadeva is the Hindu deity associated with love, desire, attraction, pleasure, and beauty. The article traces his evolution from abstract concept to fully personified god across Hindu sacred texts.

## Names and Meanings

Kamadeva has numerous appellations reflecting his divine functions:

- **Kama**: "passion" or "longing," particularly sensual desire
- **Manmatha**: "one who agitates mind"
- **Madana**: "one who intoxicates with love"
- **Ananga**: "he who is without a body" (after incineration by Shiva)
- **Kandarpa / Darpaka**: "the inflamer"
- **Mara**: "he who wounds"
- **Kushumeshara**: "he whose arrows are flowers"
- **Ratikanta / Ratipati**: "husband of Rati"

## Birth Story Variants

### Early Scriptural Version

In the Taittiriya Brahmana and Mahabharata, Kamadeva is the son of Dharma (righteousness personified) and his wife Shraddha or Lakshmi, making him one of three divine sons alongside Shama (tranquility) and Harsha (joy).

### Puranic Account (Most Developed)

Most developed texts describe him as a mind-born son of Brahma. After creating agents of creation and Sandhya (a maiden), an exceptionally handsome youth emerges from Brahma's mind. Brahma names him Kama and commands him to "spread love in the world by shooting his flower arrows." Kama immediately tests these arrows on Brahma himself, causing the creator to become attracted to Sandhya. Dharma, witnessing this transgression, summons Shiva, who observes the chaos and laughs. Brahma recovers and curses Kama to eventual incineration by Shiva's third eye, though he promises resurrection.

### Alternative Puranic Version

Some texts state Brahma created Kama specifically to ignite passion among the prajapatis (creation agents) who had refused to procreate.

## Family Structure

**Consort:** Rati (goddess of pleasure and sensual desire) serves as his primary consort. According to the Kalika and Shiva Puranas, she emerged from a sweat drop of Daksha, assigned by Brahma to become Kama's wife. The Brahmavaivarta Purana offers an alternate origin — Sandhya died after Brahma desired her but was resurrected as Rati by Vishnu.

**Secondary Consort:** Priti (affection) appears in the Skanda Purana as a second spouse, though other texts treat this as merely an epithet of Rati.

**Children:** Kama and Rati typically have two sons — Harsha (Joy) and Yashas (Grace) — though the Vishnu Purana mentions only Harsha.

**Chief Assistant:** Vasanta, the personification of spring, was created by Brahma to aid Kama. He is described as adorned with ashoka leaves and pomegranate flower earrings.

**Attendants:** Kama leads a violent group of ganas called the Maras. He also commands the celestial apsaras (nymphs), whom Indra frequently dispatches to disrupt ascetic practices.

## The Incineration Myth: Complete Account

### Context and Necessity

The demon Taraka possessed a boon ensuring only Shiva's son could defeat him. The gods faced catastrophe because Shiva, grieving his first wife Sati's death, had withdrawn into intense asceticism, making procreation impossible. Indra and the gods summoned Kama to break Shiva's meditation and inspire love for Parvati (Sati's reincarnation and daughter of the mountain god Himavat).

### Kama's Hesitation and Preparation

Though aware this mission would result in his destruction, Kama reluctantly accepted after Indra assured him the task was essential for cosmic survival. He prepared carefully, assembling his sugarcane bow stringed with buzzing bees and five floral arrows, each inducing specific emotional states. He traveled with Rati (his consort) and Vasanta (spring personification).

### The Attack Strategy (Matsya Purana Version)

Kama employed a calculated, multi-stage assault on Shiva's meditation. He began by destabilizing Shiva's mind, introducing feelings of envy, hatred, and anger — emotions antithetical to spiritual equilibrium. Simultaneously, sensory elements aligned: bees buzzed around the meditating god, fragrant flowers bloomed, and gentle breezes flowed, all designed to distract and seduce.

After infiltrating Shiva's consciousness, Kama escalated by drawing his most potent arrow — the "Mohana" (delusion/infatuation) arrow — and firing it directly at Shiva's heart. This momentarily stirred desire in the god for Parvati.

### Divine Retaliation

Shiva quickly regained composure, recognizing his ascetic control was threatened. Enraged, he opened his third eye, from which fire emerged, instantly reducing Kama to ashes. The god of desire was annihilated.

### Aftermath and Transformation

Rati was devastated, smearing Kama's ashes across her body in mourning. She petitioned Shiva for restoration. Moved by her devotion, Shiva granted a remarkable compromise: Kama would resurrect, but no longer in embodied form. He would become **Ananga** ("the bodiless one") — invisible yet perpetually present as an unseen force influencing desire throughout existence.

### Variations in Other Texts

The **Shiva Purana** depicts Kama as more arrogant, with his arrows failing to fully penetrate Shiva's concentration, demonstrating the supreme yogi's dominance. However, Shiva absorbed Kama into himself, making him one of his ganas (attendants), symbolizing desire's presence under disciplined spiritual control.

The **Vamana Purana** portrays Shiva as emotionally vulnerable, grieving Sati's loss when Kama strikes. Overwhelmed by combined desire and grief, Shiva flees into forests, causing wives of forest sages to become infatuated with him, illustrating desire's uncontrollable nature even among the gods.

The **Kalika and Shiva Puranas** attribute incineration to Brahma's curse, since Kama shot arrows at Brahma and the other mind-born sons, causing incestuous attraction to Sandhya. Maharshis became similarly afflicted, spawning the Pitrs (ancestors) from their perspiration. Sandhya, ashamed, committed suicide and reincarnated as Arundhati.

## Resurrection Accounts

**Shiva Purana:** Shiva resurrected Kama upon his marriage to Parvati.

**Lalitopakhyana** (Brahmanda Purana section): Kama was resurrected from the eyes of goddess Lalita in Kanchipuram, where she resides as Kamakshi.

**Kalika Purana:** Goddess Kamakhya (related to Lalita) resurrected him, restoring his corporeal form at Kamarupa. Grateful, Kama constructed the first Kamakhya shrine.

**Additional Note:** The Lalitopakhyana records that from Kama's incinerated remains arose the asura Bhandasura.

## Incarnations

According to the Garuda Purana, Kama incarnates across multiple forms:

- **Pradyumna**: Son of Krishna and Rukmini (most prominent incarnation)
- **Samba**: Another son of Krishna
- **Sanat Kumara**: Son of Brahma
- **Skanda**: Son of Shiva (also called Kartikeya)
- **Sudarshana**: Presiding deity of Sudarshana Chakra
- **Bharata**: The Mahabharata figure

## Iconography and Visual Depiction

### Physical Description

The Shiva Purana describes Kama as the pinnacle of masculine beauty: a golden complexion, broad muscular chest resembling a doorframe, well-proportioned limbs including plump thighs and calves, and a slender waist with deep navel. His entire body emanates the intoxicating fragrance of an elephant in musth. Facial features include a straight, delicate nose; lotus-petal-shaped eyes tinged red; thick, expressive eyebrows; a moon-like face framed by soft blue-black hair; flawless teeth; and a conch-shaped neck. His gaze is described as "highly seductive," and his breath carries natural fragrance. He wears blue garments.

### Weapons and Attributes

- **Bow**: Constructed of sugarcane, stringed with a thread of humming bees
- **Five Flower Arrows**: Tipped with ashoka blossoms, mango flowers, jasmine, blue lotus, and white lotus; each induces distinct emotional states
- **Mount (Vahana)**: A colorful parrot, representing playful affection and the connection between love and speech
- **Standard**: Features a makara (mythical aquatic creature), symbolizing fertility and primal desire
- **Attendants**: Often depicted with apsaras (celestial nymphs) and gandharvas (heavenly musicians)

### Additional Symbols

A cuckoo bird, humming bees, the spring season, gentle breezes, and celestial nymphs complete his symbolic environment. The ashoka tree, often planted near temples, is dedicated to Kama and represents love.

### Sculptural Representations

Notable examples include:
- A sixth-century sculpture from Bijapur
- A Metropolitan Museum stone sculpture showing Kama and Rati in tender embrace beneath flowering canopy leaves
- An ancient terracotta murti in the Mathura Museum
- Vividly painted six-foot wooden statues at the Salar Jung Museum in Hyderabad
- Sculptures at Khajuraho depicting Kama and Rati
- A bust from ancient Kashmir, circa eighth century CE
- Relief carvings on temple walls, including the Chennakesava Temple in Somanathapura (circa 1269 CE)

### Textual Prescriptions (Silpa Shastras)

According to the Silpa Shastras (architectural treatises), Kama should be adorned with floral garlands and gold ornaments, holding a sugarcane bow in his left hand and flower-tipped arrows in his right. He should be flanked by Rati and Priti, accompanied by Vasanta, with a horse-faced standard-bearer carrying a makara-emblazoned banner. However, artists typically deviate from these norms, typically depicting only Kama with Rati, a horse-faced flag-bearer, gandharvas, and apsaras.

## Role in Sacred Texts

### Vedas

In the Rigveda (particularly 10.129, the Nasadiya Sukta or Hymn of Creation), kama emerges as a primordial cosmic force — "the first seed of mind" — essential to the cosmogonic process, linking non-existence with existence through mental conception. Scholar Franklin Edgerton interprets this as a "cosmic Will" or impersonal evolutionary force.

The Atharvaveda (hymn 9.2) invokes Kama to grant desires and eliminate rivals, describing him with titles like "first seed of mind" (manoja). Hymn 19.52 honors Kama as a bestower of prosperity and success. Atharvaveda 3.25 illustrates Kama with arrows that "pierce the heart" — the first identifiable characteristics of the god depicted in later literature.

### Brahmanas

In these texts, kama primarily denotes generic desire within sacrificial contexts. The Shatapatha Brahmana contains the formulaic expression: "Desire has given to desire; desire is the giver, desire the receiver." The Taittiriya Brahmana, however, depicts Kama as a deity, son of Dharma and Shraddha.

### Mahabharata

Kama appears under names including Kandarpa, Manmatha, Smara, and Madana, functioning both as mythological deity and personified philosophical concept. The Adi Parva firmly establishes him as the son of Dharma and husband of Rati. The Kamagita verses present him as simultaneously immortal and indestructible: "None can destroy me… I am the one immortal and indestructible." Victims of desire are "struck by the sharp arrows of Manmatha," emphasizing emotional and psychological impact. Exceptionally attractive men, such as the hero Nala, are described as physical embodiments of Kamadeva, emphasizing his role as a symbol of irresistible beauty.

The Udyoga Parva locates Kama in the northern direction, Shiva and Uma's domain at Mount Kailash, where his presence stirs divine passion. The Shanti Parva presents Kama allegorically as Virupa ("ugly") in philosophical debate. The Anushasana Parva elevates Kama as a dharanīdhara (upholder of the earth) and associates him with sankalpa (mental intention), listing him among seven cosmic sustainers. Mahabharata 13.84.11 equates Kama with sankalpa, describing him as "everlasting intention" connected to Rudra's vigor and Skanda's birth myth.

### Ramayana

Kamadeva (under names Kandarpa and Manmatha) retains his role as divine embodiment of love and desire. His power to "disturb minds and kindle longing" is evident in epithets like Manmatha ("mind-disturber") and Madana ("intoxicator"). Rama, after Sita's abduction, is described as "kāmavaśaṃgata — overpowered by love" and "perpetually absorbed in thought and sorrow." The epic alludes to Kama's incineration and his transformation into Ananga ("the bodiless one"), emphasizing his continued power despite physical destruction. Rati explicitly appears as Madana's wife, solidifying their mythological pairing.

### Puranas

By the seventh century CE, Kamadeva was well-established in Indian oral and literary traditions. He is identified as "lord" (prabhu) of the Gandharvas and Apsarasas, positioning him as ruler of divine beings emblematic of beauty and seduction. The Harivamsha identifies Pradyumna (Krishna's son with Rukmini) as Kama's manifestation and Aniruddha (Pradyumna's son) as "son of Rati," extending Kamadeva's presence into Krishna's dynastic narrative.

## Worship and Beliefs

### General Devotion

The deity and consort Rati are included in the pantheon of Vedic-Brahmanical deities like Shiva and Parvati. Hindu marriage ceremonies often feature bride's feet painted with pictures of Suka, Kama's parrot vahana. Religious rituals addressed to Kama offer purification and community reentry, keeping desire within the religious tradition's framework. Devotees seek his intervention for health, physical beauty, spouses, husbands, wives, and sons.

### Festivals and Rituals

**Holi**: This major Hindu festival is sometimes called Madana-Mahotsava or Kama-Mahotsava. Jaimini mentions it in early writings like Purvamimamsa-sutra (circa 400 BC).

**Ashoka Trees**: Planted near temples, these trees symbolize love and are dedicated to Kamadeva.

### Gaudiya Vaishnava Tradition

In this sect, Krishna is identified as the original Kamadeva in Vrindavana. Kamadeva also incarnates as Krishna's son Shamba after Shiva's incineration. A distinction exists between the celestial demigod Kama (capable of inducing lusty desires) and the spiritual Kamadeva. Krishna is understood as Kama's source — the "ever-fresh transcendental god of love of Vrindavana," origin of all Kamadeva forms, yet transcending mundane love.

When Kamadeva/Smara appears in Bhagavata Purana's Krishna narratives regarding gopis (cowherd maidens), he doesn't represent the lust-inducing deva. Rather, smara refers to Krishna himself, whose flute increases influence on devoted gopis. The symptoms experienced — attraction through eyes, intense mental attachment, determination, sleep loss, emaciation, external disinterest, shamelessness, madness, stunning, and death — represent the ten stages of Cupid's effects.

### Indonesian Context

Kamadeva appears in the twelfth-century Javanese poem Smaradahana, rendering the myth of his burning by Shiva and descent to earth. Kakawin poetry and later Wayang narratives reference Kama and Rati as Kamajaya and Kamarati.

## Temples

Contrary to common belief, dedicated Kamadeva temples are virtually non-existent, and market-sold murtis for worship are rare. However, he frequently appears in illustrations, especially in South Indian Shiva temples depicting his incineration.

Related temples include:
- **Kameshwara Temple, Aragalur**: Local Sthala purana indicates Kamadeva awakened Shiva at this location
- **Kameshvara Temple, Kamyavan**: One of twelve Vrindavana forests
- **Soundaraja Perumal Temple, Thadikombu**: Near Dindigul, Tamil Nadu
- **Harsat-Mata Temple, Abhaneri**: Contains Kamadeva representation

## Kamadeva in Jainism

In Jain universal history, Kamadeva is not a single god but rather a title applied to twenty-four exceptionally handsome men in the current descending half-cycle (avasarpiṇī). Jain sources distinguish these from the 63 salakapurusas, though three — Shantinatha, Kunthunatha, and Aranatha — appear among the 24 Tirthankaras.

According to Jain literature Tiloya Panatti, all Kamadevas become ascetics during their lifetimes and attain moksha (liberation) within the same birth or within one to two subsequent births.

The 24 Kāmadevas include Bahubali (1st Tirthankara's son, famed for renunciation), various kings and heroes (Nalaraja identified with King Nala), divine figures like Hanuman and Pradyumna, and notably Jambusvami, the last Kevali (omniscient) disciple revered across sectarian traditions.

## Comparative Deities

Kamadeva parallels Eros (Greek) and Cupid (Roman) in their respective pantheons as the divine personification of romantic and erotic love.

# ARTICLE 6: DAKSHA YAJNA

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daksha_yajna

## Overview

The Daksha yajna represents a pivotal Hindu mythological event documented across multiple Puranic texts. This narrative describes "a yajna (ritual-sacrifice) organised by Daksha, where his daughter, Sati, immolates herself," followed by Shiva's destructive retribution.

## Background

### Sati-Shiva Marriage

Daksha, a Prajapati and Brahma's son, married Prasuti (also called Asikni). Their youngest daughter Sati "was deeply in love with the destroyer deity, Shiva, and wished to become his wife." Daksha opposed this union because he considered Shiva's lifestyle — living among the downtrodden, wearing tiger skin, and dwelling in graveyards — incompatible with royal status. Sati pursued severe austerities (tapas) and eventually married Shiva despite her father's disapproval.

### Brahma's Yajna

During a massive sacrifice conducted by Brahma, all deities and kings attended. When Daksha arrived, everyone except Brahma and Shiva stood to honor him. Brahma remained seated as Daksha's father; Shiva stayed seated due to his superior stature. Daksha interpreted Shiva's composure "as an insult" and resolved to seek revenge.

## The Ceremony

Daksha organized a grand yajna specifically to insult Shiva, deliberately excluding him and Sati from the invitation. According to the Skanda Purana, even Brahma and Vishnu declined attendance despite being invited.

### Dadhichi-Daksha Argument

The sage Dadhichi noticed "there was no sacrificial portion (Havvis) allotted to Shiva and his wife, and no Vedic hymns were used in the yajna addressing Shiva." He warned Daksha against altering Vedic practices for personal grievances. Dadhichi and supporting priests objected, then departed.

### Death of Sati

Sati learned of the yajna and requested Shiva's attendance. He refused, explaining invitations were customary. Sati insisted she needed no formal invitation as Daksha's favorite daughter, so Shiva reluctantly allowed her to attend without him.

Upon arrival, Daksha avoided interaction and "repeatedly snubbed her in front of all the dignitaries." He publicly insulted Shiva, calling him "an atheist, and a cremation ground dweller." Sati felt "deep remorse for not listening to her beloved husband" and "unable to bear further humiliation, Sati took her own life by jumping into the sacrificial fire." Alternative versions describe her transforming into Durga or Adi Shakti before immolating herself. The assembled guests "were only able to retrieve the half-burnt body of Sati."

### Destruction of the Yajna by Shiva

Learning of Sati's death, Shiva's grief "grew into a terrible anger." His rage intensified so dramatically "that he plucked a lock of hair from his head and smashed it on the ground, breaking it into two with his leg." Two fearsome beings emerged: Virabhadra and Bhadrakali (Rudrakali). Shiva commanded them to kill Daksha and demolish the yajna.

The Vayu Purana catalogs specific injuries inflicted:
- Saraswati and Aditi's noses were severed
- Yama's staff was broken
- Mitra's eyes were removed
- Indra was trampled
- Pushan's teeth were knocked out
- Chandra was heavily beaten
- All Prajapatis were beaten
- Bhrigu's beard was cut off

Three textual accounts vary on the conclusion:

1. The **Linga Purana and Bhagavata Purana** describe: "Daksha was caught and decapitated, and the attack culminated when the Bhutaganas started plucking out Bhrigu's white beard as a victory souvenir."

2. The **Vayu Purana** states Daksha "took the form of an antelope, and jumped towards the sky. Virabhadra captured and decapitated him. Daksha begs mercy from the Parabrahman (the Ultimate Reality in Hinduism), who rises from the yajna fire, and forgives Daksha."

3. The **Harivamsha Purana and Kurma Purana** depict conflict between Vishnu and Shiva/Virabhadra, with Brahma eventually brokering peace between the deities.

## Aftermath

According to the Bhagavata Purana, Brahma and Vishnu approached the grieving Shiva, requesting he restore order and allow yajna completion. Shiva consented. "Daksha was absolved by Shiva, and the head of a goat was fixed on the decapitated body of Daksha, restoring his life."

In Shakta texts, Shiva carried Sati's corpse throughout the universe in profound grief. "Vishnu cuts Sati's corpse according to Vaishnava Puranas, whose parts fell on the places Shiva wandered." These locations became Shakta pithas — sacred shrines for the goddess. (In Shaiva accounts, her body disintegrated on its own as Shiva wandered.)

Shiva eventually withdrew from the world until "Sati reincarnated as Parvati, the daughter of the King Himavana." Like her predecessor, Parvati performed austerities and renounced royal privilege. After testing her devotion, Shiva recognized Sati's reincarnation and married Parvati.

## Shakta Pithas

The legend establishes "Shakta pithas...the sacred abodes of Devi in Shaktism" across South Asia, primarily in India and Bangladesh, with additional shrines in Pakistan, Nepal, and Sri Lanka. The Puranas reference 54 Shakta pithas (corresponding to Sanskrit alphabet letters), though only 51 remain publicly known; three remain secret.

Eighteen major Maha Shakta pithas are listed, including:
- Varanasi Pitha (Vishalakshi)
- Ujjain Pitha (Mahakali)
- Kolhapur Pitha (Mahalakshmi)
- Kamakshi Temple in Kanchipuram

## Commemoration

Several sites claim to commemorate the yajna:
- Kottiyoor in Kerala
- The Aami Mandir in Bihar
- The Daksheswara Mahadev Temple in Uttarakhand (Kankhal)
- Draksharama in Andhra Pradesh

The Kottiyoor Vysakha Mahotsavam — a 27-day ceremony conducted annually — specifically commemorates the event.
