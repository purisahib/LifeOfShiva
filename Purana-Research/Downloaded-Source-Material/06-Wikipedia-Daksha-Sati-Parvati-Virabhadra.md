# DAKSHA
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daksha

**Daksha** (Sanskrit: दक्ष, IAST: Dakṣa) is a Hindu deity whose role transformed significantly from Vedic to Itihasa-Puranic mythology. The name means "able, dexterous, or honest one."

## Etymology and Textual History

In the Rigveda (2nd millennium BCE), Daksha appears as an Aditya—"son of the goddess Aditi"—associated with skilled sacrificial actions. The Brahmanas (900-700 BCE) identify him with the creator deity Prajapati. Key iconographic elements, including his yajna and ram head, first appear in the Taittiriya Samhita of the Yajur Veda. The epics Ramayana and Mahabharata mention Daksha, though most detailed stories come from the Puranas (3rd-10th century CE).

## Legends

### Birth

The Mahabharata describes Daksha and his wife emerging from the right and left thumbs of creator god Brahma. The Matsya Purana states Daksha, Dharma, Kamadeva, and Agni were born from Brahma's right thumb, chest, heart, and eyebrows respectively. According to the Bhagavata Purana and other texts, Daksha was born twice—first as a mind-created son of Brahma, then as a son of Prachetas and Marisha. The Rig Veda uniquely states that Daksha and goddess Aditi emerge from one another, making him both her son and father.

### Consorts and Children

Daksha married Prasuti in his first birth and Asikni in his second. Prasuti, daughter of Svayambhuva Manu, bore him 16, 27, or 60 daughters. Asikni (also called Panchajani or Virani), daughter of Prajapati Virana, became his second wife after he performed penance at the Vindhyas, whereupon Vishnu granted her to him.

#### Sons

Daksha and Asikni produced five thousand sons called Haryashvas, interested in populating Earth but persuaded by Narada to pursue worldly knowledge instead and never returned. They then produced another thousand sons, the Shabalashvas, who met similar fates. An angry Daksha cursed Narada to perpetually wander.

#### Daughters

Puranic texts vary in the number of Daksha's daughters. According to the Mahabharata, Devi Bhagavata Purana, Brahma Vaivarta Purana, and Vishnu Purana, Daksha fathered 60 daughters from Asikni:

- Ten daughters (Maruvati, Vasu, Jami, Lamba, Bhanu, Urjja, Sankalp, Mahurath, Sadhya, Vishva) married Dharmadeva, god of Dharma
- Thirteen daughters (Aditi, Diti, Danu, Kashtha, Arishta, Surasa, Surabhi, Tamra, Krodhavasha, Sarama, Timi, Ira, Muni) married sage Kashyapa
- Twenty-seven daughters representing the Nakshatras (constellations) married Chandra, the moon god
- Four daughters married sage Arishtanemi
- Two married sage Bahuputra
- Two married Angiras
- Two married Krisasva

The Vishnu Purana lists 24 daughters from Prasuti, while the Linga Purana and Padma Purana mention 60. Prasuti's daughters represent virtues of mind and body and married various deities and sages:

- Thirteen married Dharmadeva (Sraddha, Lakshmi, Dhriti, Thushti, Pushti, Medha, Kriya, Buddhi, Lajja, Vapu, Shanti, Siddhi, Kirti)
- Khyati married sage Bhrigu
- Sambhuti married Marichi
- Smriti married Angiras
- Priti married Pulastya
- Kshama married Pulaha
- Sannati married Kratu
- Anasuya married Atri
- Urjja married Vashishtha
- Svaha married Agni, god of fire
- Svadha married Kavi of the Pitrs class
- **Sati married Shiva**

The goddess Rati is also considered Daksha's offspring, emerging from his sweat when Brahma asked him to provide a wife for Kama, the love god.

### Cursing Chandra

Puranic texts portray Daksha as responsible for lunar cycles. Chandra married twenty-seven of Daksha's daughters, representing the Nakshatras. Favoring Rohini, Chandra spent most time with her, making the other twenty-six sisters jealous. They complained to their father, who initially tried persuading Chandra. When that failed, Daksha cursed the lunar deity to become ill and lose brightness. Since Chandra governs vegetation, plants began dying. The devas pacified Daksha, who then decreed that Chandra would suffer illness each fortnight, gradually recovering—explaining the moon's waning and waxing. In another version, Shiva partially cured Chandra's illness, prompting Chandra to construct the Somnath temple.

### Daksha Yajna

The Daksha Yajna represents an important turning point in Hindu creation mythology and sectarian development. Sati, typically described as Daksha's youngest daughter, had always wished to marry Shiva. Daksha initially forbade it but reluctantly allowed the union.

Daksha later organized the Brihaspatistava Yajna and intentionally excluded Shiva and Sati from invitations. Despite Shiva's counsel against attending a ceremony where they weren't invited, parental bonds led Sati to go alone. Daksha snubbed and insulted her publicly before guests. Unable to endure further humiliation, Sati ran into the sacrificial fire and immolated herself.

Upon learning of the tragedy, Shiva invoked Virabhadra and Bhadrakali by plucking a lock of hair and thrashing it on the ground. Virabhadra and the Bhutaganas marched south, destroying the ceremony grounds. Daksha was decapitated, and the ritual site devastated. Bhrigu, the chief priest, invoked the Ribhus to fight the Ganas, but was tied to a pillar with his beard forcibly plucked. According to Horace Hayman Wilson, Vahni's hands were cut, Bhaga's eyes plucked out, Pusha's teeth broken, Yama's mace broken, goddesses' noses cut, Soma pummelled, and Yajneshwara (the Indra of Swayambhuva Manvantara) decapitated while attempting escape in deer form. Daksha also tried escaping but Virabhadra seized and beheaded him, throwing the head into the fire before returning to Kailasa.

Shiva forgave and resurrected Daksha with a goat's head. Bhrigu and others had their parts restored. With Vishnu as chief priest, Daksha offered sacrifice shares to Shiva, successfully completing the ritual.

## Iconography

Daksha is depicted as a man with a stocky body and handsome face or with a goat head, reflecting his resurrection after the Daksha Yajna incident.

## Genealogy

- **Vedic Parent:** Aditi
- **Itihasa-Puranic Parent:** Brahma
- **Consorts:** Prasuti (first birth), Asikni (second birth)
- **Affiliation:** Vedic: Adityas; Itihasa-Puranic: Prajapati, Manasaputra
- **Primary Texts:** Rigveda, Brahmanas, Taittiriya Samhita, Ramayana, Mahabharata, Purana

# SATI (HINDU GODDESS)
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sati_(Hindu_goddess)

**Sati** (/ˈsʌtiː/, Sanskrit: सती, IAST: Satī, lit. 'truthful' or 'virtuous'), also known as **Dakshayani** (Sanskrit: दाक्षायणी, IAST: Dākṣāyaṇī, lit. 'daughter of Daksha'), is the Hindu goddess of marital felicity and longevity, and is worshipped as an aspect of the mother goddess Shakti. Sati was the first wife of Shiva, the other being Parvati, who was Sati's reincarnation after her death.

The earliest mentions of Sati are found in the time of the Ramayana and the Mahabharata, but details of her story appear in the Puranas. Legends describe Sati as the favourite child of Daksha, who marries Shiva against her father's wishes. Later, when Daksha organises a yajna (fire-sacrifice) in which he doesn't invite her and her husband, Sati goes to attend it, only to be humiliated by her father. She then immolates herself to protest against him. In Hinduism, both Sati and Parvati, successively play the role of bringing Shiva away from ascetic isolation into creative participation with the world.

Sati's story plays an important part in shaping the traditions of two of the most prominent sects of Hinduism — Shaivism and Shaktism. After Sati's death, Shiva carried her body around the world and started performing Tandava, the celestial dance of destruction. As he did so, the other deities requested Vishnu to stop this and he did so by using his Sudarshana Chakra which divided Sati's body parts falling on the ground at 51 different places. These places are now known as Shakti pithas, and they are sacred to Hindus.

## Etymology

The name "Satī" means "truthful", "virtuous" or "noble" in Sanskrit. The word is derived from the "sat" which means "truth". She is known by various patronymics, though these names can be used for any of the daughters of Daksha. Some of these names include Dakshayani, Dakshakanya and Dakshaja.

## History and Textual Background

According to scholars William J. Winkins and David R. Kinsley, the Vedic scriptures (2nd millennium BCE) do not mention Sati-Parvati but hint to two goddesses associated with Rudra — Rudrani and Ambika. In the Kena Upanishad, a goddess called Uma-Hemavati appears as a mediator between the gods and the Supreme Brahman. Both the archeological and the textual sources indicate that the first major appearances of Sati-Parvati were during the period of the Ramayana and the Mahabharata (1st millennium BCE).

The Mahabharata mentions the destruction of Daksha yajna, the birth of Kartikeya, defeat the Asura Taraka as well as some plays between Shiva and Uma (Parvati). Scholars believe that by the time of the Puranas (c. 4th - 13th century), legends of Sati and Parvati rose to prominence and these were adapted by Kalidasa in his epic poem Kumarasambhavam (c. 5th - 6th century). Some of the Puranas which narrate Sati's story are the Vayu Purana, the Skanda Purana, the Bhagavata Purana, the Kurma Purana, the Padma Purana, the Linga Purana, the Shiva Purana, and the Matsya Purana.

## Legends

### Birth and Early Life

Sati was the daughter of Daksha—A Prajapati (agent of creation) and the son of the creator god Brahma—and Prasuti—the daughter of Manu. In some alternate accounts found in the Shiva Purana, Matsya Purana and Kalika Purana, her mother is mention to be Asikni. Sati is often mentioned as the youngest and the most beloved daughter of Daksha. According to the Shakta (goddess-oriented) texts including Devi Bhagavata and the Mahabhagavata Purana, before her birth, Brahma advised Daksha to meditate upon the Great goddess and convince her to take an avatar as their daughter (Sati). The goddess agreed but warned that if he mistreated her, she would abandon her body.

Even as a child, Sati adored the tales of Shiva and grew up an ardent devotee. As she grew to womanhood, the idea of marrying anyone else, as intended by her father, became unfair to her. It is believed that Brahma intended to get Sati married to Shiva and bring him into worldly affairs.

### Marriage

Sati is described to be very beautiful but the legends emphasize her penance and devotion, which won the heart of the ascetic Shiva. According to the legend, Sati left the luxuries of her father's palace and retired to a forest to devote herself to austerities of a hermetic life and the worship of Shiva. She was often tested by him or his attendants. Finally, Shiva acceded to her wishes and consented to marry. Despite Daksha's unwillingness, the wedding was held in due course with Brahma serving as the priest. Sati moved with Shiva in Kailash. Tension between Shiva and Daksha further arises when Daksha starts to dislike Shiva because of Shiva's odd appearance and behaviour.

According to the Bhagavata Purana, Daksha arranged Sati's svayamvara (self-choice ceremony), where all except Shiva were invited. When Sati did not find Shiva, she threw a garland in the air to choose her husband. Shiva manifested there and it fell on him, thus they were married. In the 18th century Svathani Katha, when Shiva asked Sati's hand in marriage, Daksha refused, claiming him unsuitable. Vishnu aided Shiva by disguising him as a sannyasi and had him marry her. While many versions of the tale mention Daksha's objections to the marriage, the Shiva Purana does not mention any harsh opposition, though he starts to develop a deep hatred after the wedding.

### Testing Rama's Divinity

In a legend from the Shiva Purana, Sati and Shiva were once wandering the earth. They passed through the Dandaka forest, where they came across Rama, accompanied by Lakshmana, searching for his wife Sita after her abduction by Ravana. Observing Rama's lament, Shiva bowed before him, following which he blessed the former with victory and revealed himself before him. Overcome by maya, Sati asked her consort to whom he had bowed. Shiva informed her that Rama was a full incarnation of Vishnu. Seeing that she was unconvinced, Shiva encouraged her to test Rama's divinity for herself. Sati did so by assuming the guise of Sita and appearing before him. Rama laughed, seeing through the goddess's disguise, wondering why she had assumed his wife's form. Her doubts fading, Sati asked Rama how he was worthy of being saluted by Shiva. Rama explained to her his true identity and circumstance and spoke to her of his devotion to Shiva, after which she praised him and returned to her consort.

### Daksha Yajna and Self-Immolation

The most prominent legend associated with Sati is her self-immolation to protest against her father. The first text to mention Daksha Yajna is the Taittiriya Samhita and it later appears in the Ramayana and the Mahabharata. The narrative of Sati's self-immolation appears in the Puranas, Tantra literature, and in Kalidasa's lyrical Kumarasambhava.

According to the most popular narrative, Daksha organized a yajna (sacrifice) to which all the deities, except Sati and Shiva, were invited. Wanting to visit her relatives, Sati sought to rationalize this omission and reasoned that as family, such formality was unnecessary. Shiva tried to stop her as he knew that Daksha would humiliate her, but when she was not convinced, he sent her with his gana attendants. Sati was received by her mother and her sisters, but Daksha was furious by her uninvited arrival and humiliated her and mocked Shiva. Wanting to break all ties with her father and uphold the honour of her husband, Sati immolated herself.

Deeply hurt by the death of his wife, Shiva performed the destructive Tandava dance. He created two ferocious deities — Virabhadra and Bhadrakali, who wreaked mayhem at the sacrificial place. Nearly all those present were felled overnight; Daksha was decapitated by Virabhadra. After that night, Shiva, who is considered the all-forgiving, restored the slain to life and granted them his blessings. Daksha was restored both to life and to kingship. His severed head was substituted with that of a goat.

There are varying accounts of this event. The Devi-Bhagavata Purana adds the reason behind Daksha's harsh behaviour. Shortly after Sati's marriage, Daksha polluted a sacred flower garland and as a result, he was cursed to hate his beloved daughter. At the sacrificial place, after Daksha discarded Sati's gifts and humiliated her, she used her cosmic powers and burnt her body. Some texts suggest that before Sati's death, Shakti promised that she will be reborn to a father who merits her respect and remarry Shiva. The Mahabhagavata Purana presents Sati as a fierce warrior. When Shiva prevented Sati from visiting the event, she transformed into the ten fearsome Mahavidya goddesses led by Kali, and surrounded him from the ten cardinal directions. Seeing his wife's powers, Shiva allowed her. Sati, transformed as Kali, went to the sacrifice and split herself into two entities — one real but invisible and another just Chhaya (shadow or clone). Chhaya Sati destroyed the sacred event by jumping into the sacrificial fire, while the "real" Sati is reborn as Parvati. The Brihaddharma Purana (c. 13th century) narrates the creation of the Mahavidyas but there is no mention of Sati splitting into two. She retains her calming nature after Shiva allowed her. The most drastic change in this text is the absence of the self-immolation of Sati. Instead, the text mentions that she cursed her father and quit her body in a Himalayan cave. The Kalika Purana does not mention Sati going to the event, instead it is found that Sati left her body using a yogic process, after her niece, Vijaya informed her about the yajna.

### Formation of the Shakti Pithas

Another important legend associated with Sati is the formation of the Shakta pithas. Shakta pithas are shrines of the Mother Goddess, believed to have enshrined with the presence of Shakti due to the falling of body parts of the corpse of Sati. It is believed that an enraged Shiva performed the Tandava dance with Sati's charred body, which led her body to come apart and the pieces fell at different places on earth. In a more detailed narration found in some texts, Shiva, crazed with grief, roamed with Sati's corpse throughout the universe, causing universal imbalance. The divinities called upon the god Vishnu to restore Shiva to normalcy and calm. Vishnu used his Sudarshana Chakra (discus weapon) to dismember Sati's cadaver, following which Shiva regained his equanimity.

The legend ends with Sati's body being dismembered into many pieces which fell on earth at various places. Several different listings of these holy places, known as Shakta pithas, are available; some of these places have become major centres of pilgrimage as they are held by the Goddess-oriented Shakta sect to be particularly holy. Besides main Shakta pithas, some small peethas like Bindudham came into existence which are due to Sati's fallen blood drops.

### Rebirth

A depressed Shiva returned to his ascetic world while Sati was reborn as Parvati, daughter of Himavat, king of the mountains and personification of the Himalayas, and his wife, Mena. Himavat appreciated Shiva ardently. Consequently, Parvati like Sati, won Shiva over by her penance and married him.

## Legacy and Worship

The legend of Daksha Yajna and Sati's self-immolation had immense significance in shaping the ancient Sanskrit literature and even had impact on the culture of India. It led to the development of the concept of Shakta pithas and there by strengthening Shaktism. Many stories in Puranas took the Daksha yajna as the reason for its origin. It is an important incident in Shaivism resulting in the emergence of goddess Parvati in the place of Sati and making Shiva a grihastashrami (house holder) leading to the origin of Ganesha and Kartikeya.

There are very few shrines in India dedicated to Sati Devi, and Kottiyoor is one of them. The ancient Kottiyoor temple in Kottiyoor, Kannur, Kerala, is one of the only places in India where Goddess Sati is worshiped as she is. Next to temple's swayambhu linga is the Ammarakallu, a raised platform with a natural rock structure surrounded by a circular stone boundary, with a brass lamp placed under a traditional palm leaf umbrella. It is believed to be where the goddess immolated herself during the Daksha Yaga. During the shiveli (a temple ritual consisting of "offering food to the members of the [deity's] retinue"), the goddess's thidambu (the decorated replica of the deity in a temple that is usually taken outside for the purposes like festivals and poojas) is taken to the Ammarakallu in a procession. The Goddess is also venerated during the famed Kottiyooor Vyshaka Mahotsavam. The Ammarakallu has also come to be associated with the Goddess Parvati, thereby representing both incarnations of the Goddess.

# PARVATI
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parvati

Parvati is one of Hinduism's principal goddesses, revered as the deity of power, energy, nourishment, harmony, love, beauty, devotion, and motherhood. She forms the Tridevi trinity alongside Lakshmi and Saraswati. She is the consort of Shiva and the mother of Ganesha and Kartikeya. She is the reincarnation of Sati, Shiva's first wife, who self-immolated at the Daksha yajna.

## Etymology and Names

The name "Parvati" derives from Sanskrit parvata meaning "mountain." She is called "she of the mountain" because she is the daughter of Himavan (Himavat), the mountain king. Alternative names include:
- Uma — a common name, one of her oldest epithets
- Gauri — the fair one
- Aparna — one who did not even eat leaves during her penance
- Shailaja — daughter of the mountain
- Annapurna — goddess of food and nourishment
- Kamakshi — goddess of love

In her fierce forms she is known as Durga, Kali, Chandika, and Mahakali.

## History and Textual Background

The word "Parvati" does not appear explicitly in Vedic literature. However, Uma-Haimavati appears in the Kena Upanishad (mid-1st millennium BCE) as a mediator between the gods and the Supreme Brahman. During the epic period (400 BCE–400 CE), both the Ramayana and Mahabharata present Parvati as Shiva's wife. Comprehensive details of her story emerge primarily in Kalidasa's Kumarasambhava and the Puranas.

Scholars William J. Winkins and David R. Kinsley note that Vedic scriptures do not mention Sati-Parvati explicitly, but hint to two goddesses associated with Rudra — Rudrani and Ambika. By the time of the Puranas (c. 4th–13th century), legends of Parvati rose to great prominence.

## Philosophical Significance

Philosophically, Parvati embodies Shiva's shakti (divine energy)—the creative cosmic force animating existence. In Shaktism, she is regarded as potentially superior to Shiva, representing the supreme feminine principle. She represents the life-affirming creative force that complements Shiva's austere, world-denying nature. Without her, Shiva is considered incomplete.

## Legends

### Birth and Childhood

Parvati is born as the daughter of Himavan (the personification of the Himalayas) and his wife Mainavati (also called Mena). She is the reincarnation of Sati. From childhood she was devoted to Shiva, listening to stories of his greatness. Her parents, especially her father Himavan, were devotees of Shiva and welcomed the idea of their daughter marrying him.

### Courtship and Penance

After Sati's death, Shiva withdrew into deep meditation and renounced the world. Meanwhile, the asura Taraka obtained a boon from Brahma making him nearly invincible, with only a son of Shiva capable of killing him. The devas were desperate — they needed Shiva to produce an heir. Brahma sent Kama (the god of love/desire) to awaken Shiva's desires while Parvati served Shiva with devotion.

Shiva opened his third eye and burned Kama to ashes with a fiery gaze. Despite this setback, Parvati refused to give up. She abandoned her royal clothes and jewels and retreated into the forest to perform intense tapas (austerities), matching Shiva's own asceticism. She ate only fallen leaves, then stopped eating altogether (earning the name Aparna — "one who ate no leaves"). Her devotion and austerities surpassed those of all other beings.

The gods, including Brahma and Indra, petitioned Shiva to accept Parvati. Shiva eventually tested her by sending seven sages (Saptarishi) to dissuade her, and then himself appeared in disguise as a young brahmin to disparage Shiva's appearance, habits, and character. Parvati remained firm and defended Shiva passionately in each test. Satisfied by her devotion, Shiva revealed his true form and agreed to marry her.

### Marriage

The marriage of Shiva and Parvati was a cosmic event celebrated by all gods and beings. Brahma performed the ceremonies. The wedding procession included Shiva's ganas (attendants), Nandi the bull, various deities, and celestial beings. The marriage took place on Mount Kailash (or in some versions, in the Himalayan region of her father's domain).

After marriage, Parvati and Shiva lived on Mount Kailash. She balanced his renunciatory tendencies with nurturing, householder values. She is the ideal wife in Hindu tradition — devoted yet strong, gentle yet powerful.

### Birth of Kartikeya

After the marriage, the devas needed their commander against Taraka. Shiva's divine seed was so potent that no womb could bear it. It was carried by Agni (fire god) and then deposited in the river Ganga. The Ganga could not hold it either and placed it among the reeds of the Sharavana (reed forest). There it manifested as six babies, who were nursed by the six Krittikas (Pleiades star-mothers). Parvati gathered all six babies into one child with six heads — this was Kartikeya (also called Skanda, Shanmukha — "six-faced", Kumara, Murugan). He later killed the demon Taraka and became the commander of the divine armies.

### Birth of Ganesha

The most popular version: Parvati, wanting a guardian of her own while Shiva was away, created a boy from the clay/turmeric paste she had rubbed from her body and breathed life into him. She stationed him at her door with instructions to let no one in. When Shiva returned, Ganesha refused him entry. Shiva's ganas fought the boy but were defeated. Shiva himself then severed Ganesha's head. When Parvati learned what had happened, she was overcome with grief and anger, threatening destruction of the universe (taking her fierce Kali form in some versions). To pacify her, Shiva sent his ganas north to find the first living being they encountered sleeping with its head pointed north. They found an elephant (or a baby elephant) and brought its head back. Shiva placed it on Ganesha's body, restored him to life, declared him his own son, and blessed him to be worshipped first before all other gods.

In another version, Shiva created Ganesha directly from his own laughter. Another account says Ganesha was created by the combined desires of Shiva and Parvati.

### Other Legends

Parvati appears in numerous other stories:
- She engaged Shiva in philosophical debates, learning the secrets of yoga, tantra, and Shaiva philosophy from him directly (these dialogues form the basis of many Tantric texts).
- She is the source of Durga, who emerged when Parvati shed her outer golden/fair skin, creating a goddess to battle the buffalo demon Mahishasura.
- She transformed into Kali in fierce moments of cosmic battle.
- She took the form of Meenakshi in South India, ruling as a warrior queen before being reunited with Shiva.
- She is worshipped as Kamakshi in Kanchipuram and Lalita Tripurasundari in Tantric traditions.

## Manifestations (Forms of Parvati)

Parvati appears in multiple forms reflecting different aspects:
- **Annapurna** — goddess of food and nourishment; feeds the universe
- **Durga** — fierce warrior-goddess defeating demons; embodied in Navadurga
- **Kali** — dark, destructive aspect destroying evil and time itself
- **Meenakshi** — regional form worshipped in Madurai, Tamil Nadu
- **Kamakshi** — goddess of love and fertility, worshipped in Kanchipuram
- **Lalita Tripurasundari** — supreme goddess in Shakta Tantra
- **Bhavani** — nourisher of the world
- **Chandraghanta** — with a crescent moon on her forehead

## Iconography

Parvati is typically depicted as fair-skinned (Gauri — the golden one) and beautiful. She wears a red sari (symbolizing power and fertility). She may have two or four arms. Common attributes she holds include:
- Trident (trishula)
- Mirror
- Rosary (japamala)
- Bell
- Flowers
- Lotus

When shown alongside Shiva in the Ardhanarishvara form (half-Shiva, half-Parvati), the right half is Shiva and the left is Parvati, symbolizing the inseparability of masculine and feminine cosmic principles. She is also symbolically represented as the yoni (feminine principle) complementing Shiva's linga (masculine principle).

## Worship and Festivals

Major festivals celebrating Parvati:
- **Teej** — celebrates married life and monsoon season; women fast and pray for husbands
- **Navaratri** — honors her nine manifestations (Navadurga) over nine days
- **Gauri Festival** — celebrates her as goddess of harvest and marital bliss
- **Gangaur** — 18-day festival particularly in Rajasthan

## Temples

Major temples dedicated to Parvati:
- Meenakshi Amman Temple, Madurai, Tamil Nadu (where she is the primary deity)
- Kamakshi Amman Temple, Kanchipuram
- Bhramaramba Temple, Srisailam (as one of the 18 Shakti Pithas)
- Various Shakti Pithas across India

# VIRABHADRA
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virabhadra

**Virabhadra** (Sanskrit: वीरभद्र, romanized: Vīrabhadra, literally 'auspicious hero') is a fierce manifestation of the Hindu deity Shiva. He emerges from Shiva's wrath when the god tears a lock of his matted hair upon learning of his consort Sati's self-immolation at the Daksha yajna.

## Legend

### Origin

According to Shaivite tradition, Virabhadra's creation stems from a tragic family dispute. Sati, the youngest daughter of Daksha, developed devotion to Shiva. During her svayamvara (marriage selection ceremony), Daksha excluded Shiva but Sati miraculously summoned him, causing Daksha to accept their union reluctantly.

Later, Daksha performed a ritual sacrifice and deliberately excluded Sati and Shiva from the invitation. Despite Shiva's objections, Sati attended due to familial affection. Upon arrival, Daksha publicly humiliated both Sati and her husband. Unable to endure this insult, Sati immolated herself in the sacrificial fire using yogic powers.

When Shiva learned of these events, he "tore a clump of his matted hair, from which Virabhadra and Bhadrakali emerged." Shiva instructed Virabhadra to devastate the sacrifice and eliminate its participants.

The Padma Purana offers an alternative account: Virabhadra arises from perspiration falling to earth as Shiva grieves Sati's death, eventually becoming Angaraka (the planet Mars) after Shiva pacifies him.

### Daksha Yajna — The Assault

The Skanda Purana describes supernatural omens preceding Virabhadra's assault — blood rain and meteor showers. Daksha sought Vishnu's protection, who agreed to help while blaming Daksha for his disrespect.

Virabhadra commanded an enormous celestial army including the Navadurga, rakshasas, yakshas, pishachas, bhutas, thousands of ganas, yoginis, and guhyakas. Depicted with three eyes, a thousand arms, great serpents, a chariot drawn by two thousand horses and a million lions, he proved devastatingly effective.

During combat, Indra struck Virabhadra with his vajra weapon. Virabhadra attempted to swallow both Indra and his mount Airavata, but Vishnu intervened. The preserver deity employed his Sudarshana Chakra against Virabhadra, who swallowed it whole before Vishnu retrieved it, claiming victory.

Continuing his rampage, Virabhadra accosted Bhrigu and Pushan. Finding Daksha cowering beneath the altar, he beheaded him and offered the head as a sacrifice. The distressed Brahma visited Shiva, pleading for the violence to cease. Shiva arrived, conversed with Virabhadra, and restored Daksha to life by placing an animal's head upon his neck. The resurrected Daksha bowed to Shiva, concluding the conflict.

Different scriptures offer varying accounts: the Kurma Purana depicts Virabhadra recognizing Vishnu and Shiva's identity; the Harivamsha and Vamana Purana show Vishnu gaining advantage.

### What Happened to the Devas

According to the detailed accounts:
- Vahni (Agni, fire god) had his hands cut off
- Bhaga had his eyes plucked out
- Pusha had his teeth broken
- Yama had his mace broken
- Goddesses' noses were cut off
- Soma (Chandra) was pummelled
- Yajneshwara (the Indra of that era) was decapitated while attempting escape in deer form
- Daksha was beheaded; his head thrown into the sacrificial fire
- Bhrigu, the chief priest, was tied to a pillar and had his beard forcibly plucked

After Shiva's forgiveness:
- Daksha was restored with a goat's head
- Bhrigu and others had their parts restored
- Vishnu served as chief priest for the completed sacrifice

### Significance

Based on the Shiva Purana, Virabhadra functions as the gotrapurusha (ancestral progenitor) of Jat communities and Veerashaiva-Lingayat Hindu subsects. These groups traditionally claim descent from Shiva's matted locks (jatta), possibly influencing the modern term "Jat." Believers regard Virabhadra as their ancestral deity, emulating his warrior qualities while opposing discrimination and ego.

The Veerashaiva-Lingayat faith established numerous temples, including the Lepakshi Veerabhadra Temple, constructed between 1530-1540 CE by brothers Virupanna Nayaka and Viranna under the Vijayanagar Empire. According to the Skanda Purana, this temple represents one of the divyakshetras (divine pilgrimage regions) of Lord Shiva.
