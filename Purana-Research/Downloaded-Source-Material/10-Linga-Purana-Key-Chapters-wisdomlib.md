## CHAPTER 17 — ORIGIN OF THE LINGA (LINGODBHAVA) {#chapter-17}

**Section 1 (Uttarabhaga), Chapter 17**
**URL:** https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/book/the-linga-purana/d/doc1195230.html
**Total Verses:** 82

### Overview

This is one of the most theologically crucial chapters of the Linga Purana — the Lingodbhava story, establishing the Linga as the supreme symbol of Shiva's infinite, formless nature. It describes the cosmic dispute between Brahma and Vishnu, the appearance of the infinite pillar of fire, and Shiva's revelation as the supreme reality beyond both.

---

### Narrative Summary

During cosmic dissolution, Brahma and Vishnu awaken in a primordial ocean. After exchanging harsh words about their respective cosmic roles, they begin fighting. To resolve their dispute, a brilliant, incomprehensible column of fire — the Linga — manifests before them. Both deities attempt to determine its boundaries: Vishnu transforms into a black boar to descend toward its root, while Brahma becomes a swan ascending toward its apex. After thousand-year searches, both fail and return humbled.

The Linga produces the sacred sound "Om," which contains the syllables 'a,' 'u,' and 'm.' These syllables represent Brahma, Vishnu, and Rudra respectively, embodying creation, sustenance, and dissolution. Through meditation on these Sanskrit letters and their associated mantras, Vishnu perceives Shiva's cosmic form, recognizing him as supreme. The Linga becomes established as Shiva's divine representation — the ultimate symbol of the formless Absolute.

---

### Verse-by-Verse Content

**Verses 1-5**
Suta concludes previous narrative about Sadyojata; promises that listeners attain Brahman-identity through grace. The chapter is framed as sacred teaching — hearing this story grants identity with Brahman itself.

**Verses 6-13**
Cosmic setup: Worldly age ends; creation withdraws after thousand yuga-cycles; all beings perish in universal drought. Vishnu sleeps in cosmic waters with thousand heads, eyes, feet, arms. He is identified with rajas, tamas, sattva as Brahma, Rudra, Vishnu respectively. He is characterized by Kala (Time); described as source of Being and non-Being.

**Verses 14-16**
Brahma perceives sleeping Vishnu, strikes him awake in anger. Vishnu rises from his serpent-couch (Shesha/Ananta) with bleary lotus-eyes.

**Verses 17-18**
Vishnu greets Brahma cordially. But Brahma's rajas-provoked arrogance immediately surfaces.

**Verses 18-32**
The great philosophical debate: Brahma claims supreme creatorship and eternity. Vishnu responds asserting his lordship — stating Brahma was born from his body, that creator/sustainer/destroyer roles are his alone, that he is responsible for Pradhana and cosmos creation. A fierce battle erupts between them in the dissolution ocean.

**Verses 33-35 — THE LINGA APPEARS**
The pivotal moment: A brilliant Linga materializes to suppress their dispute. It is described as having "thousands of clusters of flames," comparable to all-consuming fires. It is stable — without increase or decrease. It lacks beginning, end, or middle. It is incomparable and inexplicable. No one has seen anything like it.

**Verses 36-38**
Both deities decide to test the Linga's limits. Vishnu assumes black boar-form for downward descent toward the root. Brahma assumes swan (hamsa) form for upward ascent toward the apex. These forms become their eternal identifiers in iconography.

**Verses 39-43**
Brahma moves with the speed of wind and mind in his upward journey. Vishnu's boar-form is described elaborately: a hundred Yojanas in length, ten in girth, blue-black in coloration, with white curved teeth, sun-like refulgence, long snout, loud grunt, short legs, diverse colors — victorious and irresistible.

**Verses 44-46**
After a full thousand years of descent, Vishnu cannot reach the root of the Linga. Brahma similarly cannot see its upper end despite tireless efforts. Both return exhausted and humbled.

**Verses 47-48**
Both bow to Shiva. Vishnu stands mentally dejected, deluded by Maya. They bow at all sides, expressing profound wonder at what they have encountered.

**Verses 49-51 — OM EMANATES FROM THE LINGA**
A loud "Om" sound emanates from the Linga — prolated, resonant, crystal-clear. They perceive 'a' on the right, 'u' on the left, 'm' in the middle, with a vibratory nada-tone completing the sacred syllable.

**Verses 52-55 — THE FOUR ASPECTS OF OM**
Vishnu sees 'a' like a sun-disc in the south; 'u' fire-like in the north; 'm' moon-like at the center. A fourth entity appears above — pure crystal in appearance — attributeless, nectarine, unsullied, undisturbed. It is void yet endowed with interior and exterior, beginningless, the very source of bliss.

**Verses 56-62**
Vedic interpretation: Three matras plus half-matra (nada) constitute Brahman. Each of the three Vedas correlates to one syllable. Vishnu meditates on Shiva through Vedic understanding. The Vedas themselves become the sage revealing Shiva's true nature.

**Verses 63-65 — THE SYMBOLIC COSMOLOGY**
Symbolic mapping of creation through Om: 'm' is the sower, 'a' is the seed, 'u' is the womb. Together with nada they represent Shiva. The sower creates the seed which is discharged into the womb — thus the entire cosmos is born from the Linga.

**Verses 66-68**
The seed from Om becomes a golden egg encasing the syllable 'a'. The cosmic egg gestates in the primordial waters for a thousand years. Then the Linga-lord himself splits the egg into two halves.

**Verse 69**
Four-faced Brahma is born from the egg. He is identified as the universe-creator and lord of three forms.

**Verses 70-72**
Yajus exponents confirm Om is identical with Brahman. Rik and Saman shrutis agree. All deities meditate and eulogize the lord through Vedic mantras. The delighted lord then assumes sound-form within the divine Linga — dwelling inside it as its living essence.

**Verses 73-82 — THE SANSKRIT ALPHABET AS SHIVA'S BODY**
The most profound cosmological passage: Each vowel and consonant of the Sanskrit alphabet represents a body part of Shiva.
- 'a' = head
- 'a' (long) = forehead
- 'i' = right eye
- 'i' (long) = left eye
- (continuing through all letters to the feet)

Five sacred mantras are then described with specific attributes:
- **Rik** — 24 syllables, Gayatri meter, green color, controlling efficacy
- **Atharvan** — 33 syllables, black color, black-magic efficacy
- **Yajus** — 35 syllables, white color, peace efficacy
- **Saman** — 66 syllables, Jagati meter, coral-red color, creation/dissolution efficacy
- **Combined form** — the synthesis of all four

Through mantra recitation, Vishnu finally perceives Shiva's complete cosmic form: with Vedic bodies, Ishana coronet, five-fold face (Tatpurusha, Aghora, Vamadeva, Sadyojata faces), serpent ornaments, omnidirectional eyes and hands. He eulogizes the supreme lord as granting creation, sustenance, dissolution, and all boons.

---

### Key Theological Significance

- The Linga is not a phallic symbol but the **infinite pillar of fire** — the formless, boundless reality of Shiva that has no beginning or end
- Even Brahma (creator) and Vishnu (sustainer) cannot comprehend Shiva's extent
- Om is Shiva's sonic form — the Linga made audible
- The Sanskrit alphabet itself is Shiva's body — language is divine
- This story explains WHY Shiva is worshipped in Linga form — it represents the incomprehensible Infinite

---

### Named Characters
- **Brahma** — four-faced creator, assumes swan form
- **Vishnu** — sustainer, also called Achyuta, Ishana; assumes boar form
- **Shiva/Rudra** — supreme lord, also Ishana, Umapati
- **Uma** — Shiva's consort, mentioned briefly
- **Suta** — narrator (Romaharshana)
- **Shesha/Ananta** — the cosmic serpent on whom Vishnu sleeps

## CHAPTER 18 — VISHNU'S PRAISE OF SHIVA (VISHNUSTAVA) {#chapter-18}

**Section 1 (Uttarabhaga), Chapter 18**
**URL:** https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/book/the-linga-purana/d/doc1195231.html
**Total Verses:** 91 (hymn) + concluding verses

### Overview

Immediately following the Lingodbhava revelation of Chapter 17, Vishnu addresses Shiva with an extended hymn of 91 verses of veneration — the Vishnustava. This is one of the longest and most comprehensive hymns to Shiva in the Linga Purana. Brahma then notes the hymn is sacred and destroys all sins. Readers or reciters gain spiritual merit and access to Brahma's realm, regardless of past transgressions.

---

### Narrative Context

After perceiving Shiva's five-fold cosmic form through mantra recitation (Chapter 17), Vishnu is filled with devotion and begins this great hymn. The hymn moves from metaphysical foundations through elemental manifestations, through cosmic functions, and concludes with universal containment.

---

### Structure of Vishnu's Praise

**Verses 1-2:** Opening obeisance to Rudra in syllabic (Eka-akshara / single-syllabled) form. Celebrates Shiva's luminosity and transcendent nature.

**Verses 3-5:** Honors Shiva's various manifestations and the sacred dwelling places where he resides.

**Verses 6-8:** Praises his pervasiveness through the five cosmic elements — gold (earth), water, wind, fire, earth. Shiva IS the elements, not merely their lord.

**Verses 9-10:** Praises his sensory manifestations — he is present in every sense and sensation — and his infinite nature that exceeds all measurement.

**Verses 11-12:** Positions him between Brahma and Vishnu as destroyer and protector simultaneously — he contains both creation and annihilation.

**Verses 13-14:** Emphasizes his white complexion (like snow, like moon, like conch) and his ash-covered form — the paradox of purity through cremation ash.

**Verses 15-17:** Celebrates his multiple forms and sacrificial attributes — he is both the sacrifice and the sacrificer, both the offering and the priest.

**Verses 18-19:** References his forms as brahmin, death-god (Yama), and divine hunter — the three social roles he embodies.

**Verses 20-21:** Invokes various names and his vehicle (Nandi the bull). Praises his cosmic movement.

**Verses 22-24:** Acknowledges his gunas (three qualities of nature), his vyuhas (cosmic emanations), and his omniscience that sees all simultaneously.

**Verses 25-27:** Recognizes his primordial nature — he existed before the cosmos and will exist after it. He is the rulership itself, not merely a ruler.

**Verses 28-29:** Honors his pure consciousness and his distinctive blue neck (Neelakantha) — the mark of his sacrifice in drinking the cosmic poison Halahala.

**Verse 30:** Celebrates his androgynous form — Ardhanarishvara — the unity of masculine Shiva and feminine Shakti in one body.

**Verses 31-34:** Recognizes his cosmic dominion and superiority over all other deities. He encompasses them all.

**Verses 35-36:** Praises his imperial status and celestial adornments — the crescent moon, the Ganga, the serpents.

**Verses 37-38:** Celebrates his Vedic authority — the Vedas came from him — and his serpent ornaments (Vasuki, Takshaka).

**Verses 39-42:** Concluding verses honoring him as the one who contains the entire universe within himself — the macrocosm within which all existence unfolds.

## CHAPTER 22 — CREATION OF RUDRAS (RUDROTPATTI) {#chapter-22}

**Section 1 (Uttarabhaga), Chapter 22**
**URL:** https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/book/the-linga-purana/d/doc1195235.html
**Total Verses:** 28

### Overview

This chapter establishes the origin story of the eleven Rudras — the divine beings who represent the vital life forces (pranas) animating all creation. It also contains a crucial theology: Shiva is the source even of Brahma's creative power, and Brahma's independent creation attempts without Shiva's grace result in failure and disaster.

---

### Narrative Summary

Lord Shiva encounters Brahma and Vishnu and blesses them. When Brahma attempts creation through penance on his own, his anger produces poisonous serpents instead of living beings. In his despair, Brahma loses consciousness and dies. From his dead body, eleven divine beings called Rudras emerge, crying out with compassion. These Rudras become identified with pranas (life-force principles). Shiva then revives Brahma, who perceives Shiva's universal forms.

---

### Verse-by-Verse Breakdown

**Verses 1-2**
Shiva becomes delighted upon hearing Brahma and Vishnu's humble words and acknowledgment of his supremacy (from the previous chapter's context).

**Verses 3-7**
Shiva questions the two deities about their purpose and identity. They acknowledge his supreme knowledge while identifying themselves as his creations — born from his cosmic will and power.

**Verses 8-9**
Shiva offers boons to both deities, satisfied with their devotion and humility.

**Verses 10-11**
Vishnu requests one boon: stable, unwavering devotion to Shiva's lotus-feet. This boon is immediately granted. (This is theologically significant — Vishnu's greatest wish is devotion to Shiva.)

**Verses 12-15**
Shiva blesses Brahma, declaring him Shiva's equal in certain cosmic functions and granting him perfect creative awareness. After this blessing, Shiva departs.

**Verses 16-17 — BRAHMA'S FAILED CREATION**
After Shiva departs, Brahma performs severe penance to create living beings. But his independent effort — without Shiva's direct participation — fails catastrophically. He creates only poisonous serpents, not the living beings he intended.

**Verses 18-20**
Brahma's anger at his failure produces tears. These tears — infused with all three humours (bile, phlegm, wind) — generate more serpents. His creation has gone completely wrong.

**Verses 21-22**
Brahma despairs completely. He loses consciousness and ultimately loses his life — dying from grief and failure.

**Verse 23 — THE RUDRAS ARE BORN**
The pivotal moment: From Brahma's dead body, eleven Rudras emerge spontaneously. They cry out with compassion and mercy — weeping for Brahma, weeping for creation, weeping with the pain of existence. Their very birth-cry names them: "Rudra" comes from this crying.

**Verse 24 — THE ETYMOLOGY OF RUDRA**
The text gives the etymological explanation: "The word Rudra is derivable from ru (to cry/scream) and dru (to move)." They are called Rudras because of their crying at birth. The Rudras then become identified with the pranas — the eleven vital life forces that animate all living beings.

**Verses 25-28**
Shiva restores Brahma's life — showing his mastery over death itself. The revived Brahma then perceives Shiva's universal forms — his multiple manifestations across all of existence — and expresses profound wonder at the infinite expressions of the divine.

---

### The 11 Rudras — Their Nature

The text in this chapter does not give individual names for the eleven Rudras. It states they emerged collectively from Brahma's body and became identified with the eleven pranas (vital forces). Other Puranic sources identify the eleven Rudras as:

1. **Manyu** (Wrath/Grief)
2. **Manu** (Mind)
3. **Mahinasa** (Great-nosed / Earth element)
4. **Mahan** (The Great)
5. **Shiva** (The Auspicious)
6. **Ritadhvaja** (Truth-bannered)
7. **Ugrareta** (Of fierce seed)
8. **Bhava** (Existence)
9. **Kala** (Time/Death)
10. **Vamadeva** (Beautiful/Left-hand God)
11. **Dhritavrata** (Steadfast in vow)

*(Names vary across different Puranic traditions — Vishnu Purana, Vayu Purana, and Bhagavata Purana each give slightly different lists.)*

---

### Etymology of "Rudra" Per This Chapter

- From Sanskrit root **ru** = to cry, to scream, to wail
- From Sanskrit root **dru** = to move, to run, to flow
- Combined meaning: "One who cries and moves" or "The Running Crier"
- Contextual meaning: They cried at birth with compassion for suffering creation
- They "move" as pranas move through the body — the vital breath that flows and animates

The footnote adds: "They became known as Rudras due to their crying" and notes the Rudras represent life principles (pranas) that activate creation.

---

### Theological Significance

- **Brahma cannot create independently** — his attempt without Shiva's direct grace produces only poison and death
- **The Rudras ARE the pranas** — they are not merely gods but the very life-force principles in all beings
- **Shiva masters death** — he revives Brahma, showing his sovereignty over Yama (death)
- **Vishnu's greatest boon** = devotion to Shiva — establishing Shiva's supremacy even over the preserver

## CHAPTER 65 — THOUSAND NAMES OF SHIVA (RUDRA-SAHASRANAMA) {#chapter-65}

**Section 1 (Uttarabhaga), Chapter 65**
**URL:** https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/book/the-linga-purana/d/doc1195278.html

### Overview

The Rudra-Sahasranama (1,008 names of Shiva) as preserved in the Linga Purana. This differs from the better-known Shiva-Sahasranama of the Mahabharata. The frame story involves King Tridhanvan learning these names from Tandin (son of Brahma), by which he attained the lordship of the Ganas.

---

### Narrative Frame Story

The sages request that Romaharshana (Suta) recount the solar and lunar races. The narrative shifts to describe King Tridhanvan, a devoted follower of Shiva who lacked wealth to perform horse sacrifices (Ashvamedha). He met Tandin (son of Brahma) — a brahmin who taught him "the thousand names of Rudra, formerly mentioned by Brahma." By reciting these names, both Tandin and subsequently the king attained "the lordship of Ganas" — becoming leaders of Shiva's celestial attendants.

The sages then request Suta to recite these "thousand and eight names of Shiva."

**Key verse (52b-54a):** "By repeating these [thousand and eight names of Shiva] he attained the lordship of Ganas."

---

## CHAPTER 106 — TANDAVA DANCE OF SHIVA {#chapter-106}

**Section 1 (Uttarabhaga), Chapter 106**
**URL:** https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/book/the-linga-purana/d/doc1196086.html
**Total Verses:** 28

### Overview

This chapter explains the mythological origin and context of Shiva's famous Tandava dance — the cosmic dance of destruction and creation. Unlike the Nataraja iconography (which shows Shiva dancing on the demon Apasmara), this chapter's Tandava is performed in a cremation ground as an act of cosmic celebration after the demon Daruka is destroyed by the goddess Kali.

---

### The Full Narrative Context

**Verses 1-2 — THE REQUEST**
The sages request an explanation of why Shiva performs the Tandava dance. The chapter introduces the demon Daruka — born of an Asura family — whose intense penance had granted him destructive power over the cosmos.

**Verses 3-7 — DARUKA'S RAMPAGE**
The demon Daruka defeats all celestial beings and gods in battle. Even Brahma, Indra, and Vishnu cannot stop him. All the Devas flee in terror. They seek refuge from multiple deities, eventually learning that the prophecy declares only a woman can destroy Daruka. The Devas approach Shiva, their last refuge.

**Verses 8-12 — THE COSMIC UNION**
Shiva agrees to help the Devas. Parvati enters his body — partially merging with him — to manifest as a warrior goddess. This supernatural union occurs on a cosmic level; even Brahma remains unaware of this profound merging of Shiva and Shakti.

**Verses 13-15 — KALI'S BIRTH**
Parvati creates her divine warrior form from the poison (Halahala) within Shiva's throat. Then Shiva generates Kali directly from his third eye — a fierce, black-necked goddess (Shyamakantha) emanating pure destructive power. Kali is the concentrated fury of both Shiva and Parvati combined.

**Verses 16-18 — KALI'S TERRIBLE FORM**
Kali's appearance is so terrifying that even the Devas and Siddhas are filled with fear. Divine attendants and Pisachas manifest around her, bearing celestial ornaments and weapons. She is surrounded by her own terrifying retinue.

**Verse 19 — DARUKA SLAIN**
Kali slays the demon Daruka at Parvati's explicit command, completely eliminating the cosmic threat. The battle itself is not described — it is over in one verse, emphasizing Kali's absolute power.

**Verses 20-21 — THE COSMIC AGITATION**
Universal agitation ensues from Kali's wrathful power — even after Daruka's death, Kali's fury continues, threatening to consume everything. The text states: "by virtue of impetuosity and the fire of anger, the entire universe became agitated." Kali's wrath now becomes the new crisis.

**Verses 22-24 — SHIVA CALMS KALI**
Shiva assumes a boy-form (bala-rupa) in the cremation ground — a small, innocent, seemingly helpless child — to pacify Kali's destructive energy. Kali, deluded by Shiva's Maya, nurses and protects the boy. In doing so, she absorbs his transformative essence and her fury begins to cool. Shiva then manifests eight physical forms (Ashtamurti) to protect sacred pilgrimage sites, channeling and cooling Kali's anger further.

**Verses 25-28 — THE TANDAVA**
With Kali pacified, Shiva performs the celebratory Tandava dance as an act of triumph and propitiation toward the goddess. The dance occurs:
- **Setting:** At dusk, in a cremation ground populated by corpses and spirits
- **Companions:** He dances "along with ghosts and leaders of goblins"
- **Posture:** His trident is held aloft as he dances
- **Quality:** The dance is described as "nectar-like" — sublime, transcendent, life-giving
- **Parvati joins:** She reciprocates with joyous dancing "in the midst of ghosts, happily along with Yoginis"

The watching Devas — Brahma, Indra, Vishnu — offer worship to both dancing forms. The crisis resolves completely into cosmic harmony. Heaven and earth are restored to order through the dance.

### The Setting of the Tandava: Key Details

- **Location:** A cremation ground (shmashana) — Shiva's sacred domain
- **Time:** Dusk — the twilight between day and night, the liminal hour
- **Companions:** Corpses, ghosts, goblins, spirits — the beings of the underworld
- **Instrument/Posture:** Trident raised aloft, the damaru drum implied
- **Mood:** Joyous celebration after cosmic victory; nectar-like, life-affirming

---

### Cosmological Significance of the Tandava

The Tandava in this chapter is not the famous Ananda Tandava (Dance of Bliss) associated with Nataraja. Instead it is the **Kali Tandava** — performed specifically:
1. To honor and propitiate the goddess Kali who saved the cosmos
2. To celebrate the destruction of evil (Daruka)
3. To demonstrate that even in the cremation ground, in the company of the dead, there is divine joy
4. To restore cosmic harmony after Kali's rage threatened all creation

The dance transforms the cremation ground — the place of death — into a stage for divine celebration. This is a core Shaiva theological teaching: **death and life are not opposites but dance partners.**
