# Lord Shiva in the Mahabharata

## Overview

The **Mahabharata** (composed c. 400 BCE – 400 CE) is not a Purana but the great Hindu epic. It references Lord Shiva extensively — as a supernatural power that intervenes in the epic's events, as the object of devotion by the Pandavas, and as the giver of decisive weapons.

The Mahabharata contains some of the most dramatic Shiva stories outside the Puranas.

---

## 1. THE KIRATARJUNIYA — Arjuna vs. Shiva in Disguise

This is one of the most important Shiva stories in all of Sanskrit literature. It also forms the basis of the classical Sanskrit poem **Kiratarjuniya** by Bharavi.

### The Context

During the Pandavas' forest exile, the great sage Vyasa advised **Arjuna** that the only way to defeat the Kauravas was to obtain divine weapons (divyastras) from the gods. Arjuna went on a solo spiritual quest — performing intense tapasya in the Himalayas to acquire weapons.

### The Test

Arjuna meditated for months. Eventually, Shiva decided to test him.

A demon named **Muka** (sent by Duryodhana) attacked Arjuna in the forest in the form of a wild boar. Arjuna shot the boar. But simultaneously, a hunter — a **Kirata** (forest dweller) with his wife — also shot the boar.

The hunter was Shiva in disguise. His wife was Parvati.

A dispute arose: *whose arrow killed the boar?*

Arjuna claimed it was his. The hunter insisted it was his.

**The Fight:** Arjuna shot arrow after arrow at the hunter. The hunter was unaffected. Arjuna's quiver emptied. He fought with his bow itself — it broke. He fought with his hands. He was exhausted.

And then he realized: *this is no ordinary hunter.*

He made a clay image of Shiva (the first Shiva linga made by mortal hands in this story tradition) and offered flowers.

The flowers landed on the hunter's feet instead — they moved by divine power.

**Shiva revealed himself.** Arjuna prostrated in awe and apology.

---

### The Pashupatastra is Given

Pleased by Arjuna's courage in fighting without retreat, and moved by his devotion, **Shiva gave Arjuna the Pashupatastra** — the Pasupata weapon.

*"This weapon, Arjuna, is the weapon of Shiva. It can be released by thought, by glance, by word, or by the bow. It has no equal. It can destroy all creation if used to its full power. Use it only against the most powerful supernatural enemies — never against ordinary humans."*

Shiva also healed Arjuna's injuries and restored his divine weapons.

After Shiva, Arjuna received weapons from Indra, Yama, Varuna, and other gods — but the Pashupatastra remained the supreme weapon he never actually fired in the war.

### Why This Story Matters
- Shows Shiva directly intervening in the Mahabharata war's preparation
- The Pashupatastra was the Pandavas' ultimate deterrent — the knowledge that they possessed it may have been part of what Arjuna needed psychologically as much as militarily
- **Shiva tested courage, not just piety** — he came in disguise to see if Arjuna would fight even when outmatched

## 2. ASHWATTHAMA — The Mark of Shiva's Curse

**Ashwatthama** was the son of Drona (the Pandava and Kaurava teacher). He was born with a jewel (mani) in his forehead — a sign of Shiva's blessing at birth.

The Mahabharata says:
*"Ashwatthama was born as an incarnation of a portion of Shiva. Drona had performed intense tapasya to Shiva asking for a son equal to Shiva himself. Shiva agreed — but only to give a part of himself. Ashwatthama was that part."*

### Ashwatthama's Role in the War

- Was one of the most formidable warriors on the Kaurava side
- After the Kaurava defeat, Ashwatthama in rage and grief committed the massacre of the sleeping Pandava camp
- He killed the five sons of the Pandavas, the children of Draupadi

### Shiva's Role in Ashwatthama's Night Attack

The Mahabharata describes that before the night massacre, **Shiva himself entered Ashwatthama** — the Shiva portion within him fully manifested:

*"Ashwatthama went to the Kaurava camp and meditated at the fire. Shiva appeared before him and said: 'Your time for this action has not yet come.' When Ashwatthama persisted, Shiva said: 'The Pandavas are protected by Vishnu/Krishna. I cannot give you power against them directly. But I will enter you and be present in you during this night.'"*

This passage shows Shiva as a complex, ambiguous force — sometimes supporting the "wrong" side for reasons beyond human understanding.

### The Curse on Ashwatthama

After the night massacre, **Krishna cursed Ashwatthama** — that his forehead jewel (mani) would be removed and he would wander in agony for 3000 years, his wounds never healing, his body covered in pus and blood.

This became the curse of Shiva's own portion — suggesting that even divine elements, when directed by ego and grief toward destruction, suffer consequences.

## 3. YUDHISHTHIRA'S PILGRIMAGE — THE JYOTIRLINGA RECOGNITION

The Mahabharata (Aranya Parva / Vana Parva) contains a list of **sacred tirthas** that Yudhishthira and the Pandavas visited during their forest exile. This list includes the 12 Jyotirlingas — one of the earliest textual references to these sacred Shiva shrines.

The narrative implies that visiting these shrines was part of the Pandavas' preparation — spiritual, not merely geographical.

---

## 4. BHISHMA PRAISES SHIVA — THE SHIVA SAHASRANAMA IN MAHABHARATA

In the **Anushasana Parva** (Book 13) of the Mahabharata, the dying Bhishma lying on his bed of arrows teaches Yudhishthira about the thousand names of Shiva.

This **Shiva Sahasranama** embedded in the Mahabharata is one of TWO major versions (the other being in the Linga Purana).

Bhishma tells Yudhishthira:
*"If you wish to attain victory, wealth, liberation, or any of the four goals of life — chant these thousand names of Shiva every day. There is nothing in the three worlds that cannot be obtained through Shiva's grace."*

**Key verse from Mahabharata Shiva Sahasranama:**
*"Namas-te Rudra manyave uto ta Ishe namas-te astu — I bow to your anger, Rudra. I also bow to your arrow. I bow to your grace, Rudra. I bow to both your arms."*

This connects directly to the Vedic Shri Rudram tradition.

---

## 5. THE UPAMANYU STORY — Devotion That Moved Shiva

The Mahabharata (Anushasana Parva) contains the story of **Upamanyu** — one of the most touching devotion stories:

Upamanyu, as a young student (brahmachari), had nothing to eat in his Guru's ashram. His mother advised him to worship Shiva for food. He went into the forest and began fasting and worshipping Shiva.

Indra appeared in Shiva's form and offered him milk and food. Upamanyu refused:
*"I am worshipping only Shiva. I will accept nothing from any other god."*

Shiva and Parvati appeared in their true forms. They were so moved by Upamanyu's single-minded devotion that:
- Shiva blessed him with immortality
- Shiva filled his entire ashram with divine food
- Shiva declared that his lineage would always have abundance

*Krishna himself tells this story to Arjuna before the Kurukshetra war — implying that Arjuna's upcoming tapasya for weapons was following the tradition of Upamanyu's devotion.*

## 6. KRISHNA AND SHIVA — The Mahabharata Perspective

The Mahabharata presents the interesting theological position that **Krishna and Shiva honor each other**:

### Krishna's Daily Worship of Shiva

*"Every morning, without fail, Krishna would rise before dawn and worship Lord Shiva with bilva leaves and flowers. He would chant the Shiva Sahasranama. Only after this worship would he begin his other activities."*

This is stated in the Anushasana Parva and creates an extraordinary scene: Vishnu's avatar (Krishna) worshipping Shiva as his supreme deity.

### Shiva's Blessing of the Pandavas

Before the Kurukshetra war began, Shiva appeared to the Pandavas in a dream and blessed them:
*"My grace is with the Pandavas. Dharma will prevail. I will be present on the battlefield."*

## 7. THE BANASURA WAR — SHIVA FIGHTS KRISHNA (Harivamsa/Mahabharata Appendix)

The **Harivamsa** (the appendix/continuation of the Mahabharata) contains the most dramatic confrontation between Shiva and Vishnu found anywhere in Hindu literature.

### The Setup

**Banasura** was a demon king with a thousand arms who was also a devoted Shiva bhakta. Shiva was so pleased with his devotion that he became Banasura's personal guardian — stationed his own army at Banasura's fort to protect him.

Banasura's daughter **Usha** fell in love with **Aniruddha** (grandson of Krishna). Aniruddha was captured by Banasura.

When Krishna came to rescue Aniruddha, he had to fight through Shiva's army — and then Shiva himself.

### The Battle

**Shiva vs. Krishna:**

Shiva's army attacked first — they were defeated by Krishna's Sudarshana Chakra.

Then Shiva himself engaged Krishna in direct battle. The fight was cosmic in scale:
- Shiva fired the Pashupatastra
- Krishna countered with Narayanastra
- Shiva used Brahmanda (universe-level) weapons
- Krishna used weapons no one had seen before

*The battle was described as evenly matched — because Shiva and Vishnu are equal in power.*

### The Resolution

Eventually, **Shiva himself stopped the battle**:

*"I will not fight further. You are Vishnu. I am Shiva. We are one. This fight has no meaning. I withdraw my protection from Banasura — his pride and arrogance have forfeited my protection. Release Aniruddha and let the young people be together."*

Shiva and Krishna embraced as brothers/equals. Banasura's arms were cut off by Krishna (Shiva had asked only that his life be spared).

**This story is unique in all of Hindu literature** — the only place where Shiva and Vishnu engage in direct combat, and the resolution shows them recognizing each other's true nature and choosing harmony.

## 8. THE SHANTI PARVA — SHIVA AS ABSOLUTE CONSCIOUSNESS

The philosophical sections of the Mahabharata (**Shanti Parva**, Book 12) contain some of the deepest discussions of Shiva's nature:

*"Shiva is the witness of all. He watches but does not interfere unless devotion calls him. He is present in the inbreath and the outbreath. He is in the space between words. He is what remains when everything is removed. The sages call him Akshara — the imperishable — and Maheshvara — the great lord."*

*"One who knows Shiva truly has already attained liberation — for the knowing itself is liberation."*

## Summary: Shiva's Role in the Mahabharata

| Episode | Where | What Shiva Does |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Kiratarjuniya | Vana Parva | Tests Arjuna in disguise; gives Pashupatastra |
| Ashwatthama's birth | Early chapters | Gives part of himself as Drona's son |
| Night massacre | Sauptika Parva | Enters Ashwatthama to enable the attack |
| Shiva Sahasranama | Anushasana Parva | Bhishma teaches 1000 names to Yudhishthira |
| Upamanyu story | Anushasana Parva | Tests and rewards pure devotion |
| Krishna's worship | Anushasana Parva | Worshipped daily by Krishna |
| Banasura war | Harivamsa | Fights Krishna, then recognizes unity |
